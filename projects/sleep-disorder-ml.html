Detecting Sleep Disorders
with Machine Learning

Personal Project  ·  Pandas · Scikit-learn · NumPy · Seaborn

Can lifestyle data predict sleep disorders? This project demonstrates how machine learning can classify the likelihood of sleep apnea and insomnia from everyday biometric and lifestyle features — stress levels, BMI, occupation, age, and more. A full pipeline from exploratory data analysis through model evaluation, delivering 93% accuracy with a stacking classifier.

Dataset

A Kaggle dataset on sleep health and lifestyle (~364 adult entries) covering three target classes: No disorder, Insomnia (difficulty falling/staying asleep), and Sleep Apnea (pauses in breathing during sleep). Features include occupation, BMI, stress level, heart rate, daily steps, sleep duration, and blood pressure.

Pipeline

01

Data Preprocessing

Handled missing values through imputation. Normalised numerical features. Encoded categorical variables. Created a new engineered feature: blood pressure categories (low / normal / elevated / high).

02

Exploratory Data Analysis

Mapped sleep disorder prevalence across occupations, BMI categories, stress levels, age groups, and gender. Identified the strongest predictors before any modelling.

03

Model Selection & Evaluation

Tested multiple classifiers and combined them using a stacking approach. Evaluated on accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score to ensure balanced performance across all three disorder classes.

Key EDA Findings

🏥

Occupation Patterns

Nurses predominantly experience sleep apnea. Teachers and salespeople more commonly report insomnia — suggesting occupational stress profiles map closely to disorder type.

⚖️

BMI as a Risk Factor

Obese individuals face significantly higher sleep disorder prevalence compared to normal or overweight groups. BMI emerged as one of the two most important features.

😰

Stress Predicts Quality

High stress levels are the strongest single predictor of poor sleep quality across all age groups and occupations.

🧬

Age & Sleep Duration

Younger individuals sleep less and report poorer quality. A slight positive correlation exists between age and sleep duration — older adults actually sleep longer on average.

"The stacking classifier achieved 93.3% accuracy — with gender and BMI as the most important predictive features. Stress management emerges as the most actionable intervention."

Major Conclusions

Stress management is the single most impactful lever for sleep health. Maintaining a normal BMI substantially reduces disorder risk. These findings are actionable: lifestyle modifications offer measurable, predictable improvements in sleep outcomes.

Limitations & Future Work

The dataset is small (~364 entries) with occupational imbalance, limiting generalisability. Missing features — sleep efficiency, dietary data, environment — would likely improve model performance. The next step is transitioning to regression: predicting sleep efficiency as a continuous variable rather than classifying disorder type.
