Why I Chose Data Analytics
After a PhD

Dr. Bhawakshi Punia  ·  Theoretical Physical Chemistry → AI & Data Science

Why would someone with years of training in theoretical physical chemistry decide to step away from academia and pursue a career in data analytics? It's a question I've been asked more than once — and one I asked myself many times before making the leap.

"I realised I wanted a career where I can see the immediate impact of my work."

What I Loved About My PhD

My doctoral years at IISER Pune were genuinely fulfilling. I built stochastic models, ran Monte Carlo simulations, and spent long hours analysing data to understand molecular processes at the most fundamental level. I loved the problem-solving — the way a complex biological phenomenon could be reduced to an elegant mathematical framework.

Presenting findings at international conferences and collaborating with researchers across institutions gave me a taste of the breadth of problems science can address. I published 10 peer-reviewed papers, including a PNAS article, all as first author. Academically, it was a success.

The Realisation: Academia Isn't the Only Path

But somewhere in the middle of my PhD, a quieter realisation was forming. Academic research, by its nature, operates on long feedback cycles. A paper takes months or years to move from idea to publication, and its real-world impact is often indirect and hard to trace.

I wanted to solve problems where I could see the outcome — where a model I built could help someone make a better decision, understand a health risk, or get a clearer answer. That desire for immediate, measurable impact is what started pulling me toward data analytics and applied machine learning.

"Data analytics offers the opportunity to see the immediate impact of my work — and that matters deeply to me."

Why Data Analytics?

The transition made more sense the closer I looked. My PhD had already made me a data analyst in many ways — I was constantly wrangling datasets, building predictive models, and extracting signal from noise. The tools were different, but the thinking was the same.

Data analytics builds directly on everything I had developed: mathematical rigour, statistical intuition, and the ability to decompose a complex problem into solvable parts. What was missing was the applied toolkit — Python for data workflows, machine learning libraries, visualisation and storytelling with tools like Tableau.

So I started learning. Not from scratch, but by bridging what I already knew into a new domain.

How I Made the Transition

I gave myself a deliberate learning agenda: master Python for data science, understand the end-to-end machine learning pipeline, and build projects that solved real problems — not hypothetical ones.

I built a tool to detect hidden sweeteners in packaged food labels using OCR and NLP. I analysed yoga video engagement patterns on YouTube using NLP and Tableau. I predicted drug potency for dopamine receptor ligands using cheminformatics and deep learning. I classified sleep disorders from lifestyle data using supervised ML.

Each project was chosen to be genuinely useful — not just a technical exercise. And each one taught me something about translating domain knowledge into a data product that could actually help someone.

What's Next?

I joined Aganitha.ai as an AI & Computational Scientist, where I get to work at exactly the intersection I was looking for: applying machine learning and computational methods to problems in biology and drug discovery. The PhD wasn't a detour — it was the foundation.

If you're a scientist considering a similar path: the skills transfer more than you think. What changes is the pace, the feedback loop, and the proximity to real-world outcomes. For me, that change was worth everything.
